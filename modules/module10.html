Module 10 - Final Project Report, Data Science Communication

Pulling nine modules of analysis into one story about what actually signals lending risk, and how to tell it clearly.

What nine modules added up to

The most valuable thing about working through so many methods on the same problem is that you start to see which findings are real and which were flukes of a single algorithm. A handful of signals kept resurfacing no matter what model I pointed at the data. Whether a borrower had an unknown checking account turned out to be the single most powerful divider, leading the splits in the decision trees, topping the linear support vector machine's weights, and ranking first in the random forest. Right behind it sat the monthly burden feature I engineered back in the first module, along with loan duration and credit amount. The fact that the same short list led in clustering, in tree splits, in regression coefficients, and in ensemble importances is what convinced me these were structural truths about the data rather than coincidences.

The clearest overall lesson is that lending risk does not live in any one variable. It emerges from a combination of account status, repayment burden, loan structure, and borrower capacity working together. Every model that did well did so by weighing several of these at once, and the model that tried to lean on a single easy pattern, the Naive Bayes text classifier defaulting to the majority class, was also the weakest. Risk is a mosaic, and you only see the picture when you step back from any single tile.

The borrowers the data kept describing

Across the unsupervised and supervised work the same kinds of borrowers kept taking shape. There was a lower burden group carrying modest loans and gentle payments. There was a stronger capacity group with high income, deep credit histories, and plenty of room to borrow. And there was a heavier obligation group shouldering large or long loans without the financial cushion to match, which is the profile that most deserves a careful second look. Clustering found these groups without ever seeing an outcome, and the classifiers leaned on the very same traits to sort borrowers by risk, so the segmentation and the prediction were really two views of one underlying structure.

The complaint data added a fourth kind of borrower to keep in mind, the one whose loan has already gone wrong. Those records are not about predicting risk so much as showing what it looks like once it arrives, which kept the whole project grounded in consequences rather than abstractions.

What the complaints made real

The CFPB data was the part that kept me honest. Mortgage complaints piled up overwhelmingly on conventional home mortgages, with FHA, VA, and HELOC products trailing, and when I read what people were actually upset about, the top issues were trouble during the payment process and simply struggling to pay. That is the same repayment pressure my models kept flagging as important, except here it was no longer a coefficient or a feature importance. It was thousands of people in a bad spot.

Holding the predictive work and the complaint work next to each other changed how I read both. A high monthly burden is a number in a model and a missed payment in someone's life, and keeping those two meanings tied together is, to me, the whole point of doing this kind of analysis on a topic that matters.

Two kinds of mistakes, both costly

Watching the confusion matrices across every classifier taught me to think hard about the two ways a model can be wrong, because in lending they are not interchangeable. A false negative is a risky borrower the model waved through, which can mean a loan that should have had more scrutiny. A false positive is a sound borrower the model flagged, which can mean someone denied credit or charged more than they should be. One protects the lender and the other protects the borrower, and chasing one too hard usually sacrifices the other. My best ensembles caught roughly four in five risky borrowers, which is genuinely useful, but they were right only about half the time when they raised a flag, so the false positives were real.

That trade off is why I would never hand any of these models the final say. The honest framing is that they are good at surfacing borrowers who deserve a closer look, and they pair best with human judgment and consistent review rather than replacing it. A model that catches most of the risk while occasionally crying wolf is a helpful assistant, not an automatic decision maker, and pretending otherwise would be the dangerous reading of these results.

Telling the story so it lands

The final piece of the course was communication, and that shaped how this whole site is built. I made an early decision to read every result two ways at once, as a machine learning outcome and as something that means something about real people, and I tried to carry that voice through every module rather than dumping metrics and moving on. The journal format let me show the work in the order I actually did it, so a reader can follow the reasoning from a raw dataset all the way to a tuned ensemble without needing to take my word for any single step.

I leaned on visuals to do the heavy lifting wherever a chart could say it faster than a paragraph, kept the tables focused on the comparisons that mattered, and tried to write for a curious reader rather than only for a grader. Good data science communication, as far as I can tell, is mostly about respecting your audience enough to be clear, to admit what the models cannot do, and to connect the numbers back to why anyone should care. That is what I hope this report manages to do.
