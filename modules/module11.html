Module 11 - Finals

Closing out the term, looking back at the whole arc of the project, and being honest about what I would do next.

Looking back at the whole arc

Reaching the end of the course and looking back over the modules, what strikes me is how much ground a single dataset can cover when you keep returning to it with new tools. The same German Credit and Lending Club records that I clustered blind in the early modules came back as the proving ground for regression, trees, Naive Bayes, support vector machines, and ensembles. Studying for finals meant revisiting that whole sequence, and seeing it laid end to end finally made the shape of the field click for me, how unsupervised methods find structure, how supervised methods predict, and how ensembles combine.

The single biggest thing the project drove home is that lending risk is rarely simple. Going in, I half expected one or two variables to act as obvious red flags. What I found instead was that the signal was spread across account status, repayment burden, loan structure, and borrower capacity, and that no model could lean on a single shortcut and do well. That lesson showed up so consistently, across so many different methods, that it stopped feeling like a result and started feeling like the actual nature of the problem.

What I would do differently

If I picked this project back up, a few improvements stand near the top of the list. The German Credit data is small and somewhat dated, so a larger and more current dataset would let me trust the patterns more and lean harder on the higher risk group rather than fighting its small size with class balancing. I would also want more genuine outcome data, since so much of Lending Club skews toward Current loans, and a fuller spread of defaults and charge offs would give the classifiers far more to learn from.

On the complaint side, I only used word counts and presence flags, which left most of the meaning in those narratives on the table. Reading the complaint text properly with real language techniques would almost certainly beat the bag of words approach that struggled in the Naive Bayes module. Beyond that I would like to compare loan types directly rather than pooling them, and I would want to take fairness and transparency seriously, checking whether the signals my models rely on treat different groups of borrowers evenly. A model that flags risk well but does so unfairly is not actually a good model, and that question deserves its own careful work.

What I am taking with me

More than any single algorithm, I am leaving this course with a healthier set of instincts. I learned to look past a tidy accuracy number to the mistakes hiding underneath it, to respect how much the early cleaning and feature choices shape everything downstream, and to stay suspicious of a model that sounds confident without being checked. The credit amount per month feature I built in the first module quietly proving its worth in nearly every model that followed taught me that thoughtful preparation often matters more than the fanciness of the method.

Mostly I am glad I picked a topic with real weight. It would have been easier to model something inconsequential, but tying every result back to borrowers and lenders kept the work meaningful from the first dataset to this final page, and that is the habit I most want to carry into whatever I build next.
